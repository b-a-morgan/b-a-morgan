    Parcel Locker Uplift

    Translating brand guidelines to the locker interfaces while rectifying accessibility issues.

    Located at over 400 locations throughout Australia, Parcel Lockers allow customers to easily collect parcels in and outside of business hours. Their use is correlated with increased customer satisfaction and first time delivery rates with reduced delivery costs. 

    An ergonomics review of the existing locker interface had identified accessibility issues. Rectifying these accessibility issues provided an opportunity to update the interface to the more recent illustration-focused brand guidelines.

    Approach

    My team ran workshops to define scope and document limitations and considerations. We used flows to document user journeys and conducted research to answer gaps in our knowledge. We employed paper prototypes to ensure the flows felt natural in practice. Only once the flows were confirmed did we invest time into completing high-fidelity designs. At the end of this process, we delivered a UI kit and specification document to an off-shore development team.

    Considerations and limitations

    The ergonomic review found issues relating to screen visibility, touch detection, vision-impaired access and wheelchair accessibility. Three different generations of locker were in active use - each with a different screen size and orientation. Furthermore, the locker software did not support video, animation or analytics - meaning we were unable to determine customer preferences from usage analytics. 

    A schematic illustrating the wheelchair-accessible areas of the locker displays.

    Observational research revealed how users authenticate

    Lacking analytics, were were unsure of the most common verification method used by customers at parcel lockers. To solve this we undertook research - observing 30 customers collecting their items and noting their verification method.

    Competitor analysis gave us an idea of 'best practice'

    We wished to understand the range of experiences offered by kiosks currently in the market, deemed our 'experience competitors'. The best performing interfaces exemplified the ‘KISS’ (Keep It Simple Stupid) paradigm. I crafted a set of best practice guidelines after analysing the best performing kiosk interfaces.

    Solutions

    Wireframes and prototypes were tested against the different sizes and orientations of each display generation. To ensure wheelchair accessibility, all content and call-to-actions were placed in the lower part of the screen. The highest-priority element was placed on the bottom right-hand side of the screen on horizontal orientation, and alongside the bottom edge of the screen for the portrait orientations.

    The same design adapted to the three generations of parcel locker.

    Reviewing the content

    Content was reviewed to prioritise the most common method used by customers. Technical jargon was removed and replaced with simple action words. Not all elements could remain in a consistent place due to size limitations, so important elements were placed in contextually relevant positions e.g. a “need help scanning?” call-to-action was located below the scanning illustration.

    Welcome screen of updated interface (left) vs old (right)

    Adapting guidelines for outdoor context

    For the final design we needed to adapt the new corporate design guidelines to be suitable to an outdoor interface. This meant larger typefaces and highly contrasted colours. Additional verbal instructions were recorded for customers with vision impairments. An accessibility consultant was hired to review the design changes before go-live.

    Sample screens from across the lodgement process.

    Results

    The update was rolled out Australia-wide at the beginning of the 2016 financial year alongside an online and retail advertising push. Deliveries to parcel lockers grew by 43% from 2015 to 2016. In 2017, deliveries to parcel lockers grew by a further 41% - far above the 10% growth seen in overall domestic deliveries. Customer feedback was monitored post go-live and was very positive.
