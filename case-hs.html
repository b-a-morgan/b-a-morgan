Australia Post

Help & Support Discovery

Making it easier for registered users to get help where and when they need it

Australia Post delivers a lot of parcels (2.8 billion parcels annually). Unfortunately, a small proportion of these deliveries are delivered late or never arrive. While most late deliveries turn up within three business days, some never do, and the experience seeking help and support lefts customers frustrated and confused. Australia Post made it their mission to focus on help and support, and we were given the directive to make it easier for registered users to get help where and when they needed it.

Overview

This project was a discovery piece - meaning that we took time to research the problem space, framed the problem to be solved, and gathered evidence. In this instance, we audited existing research and undertook new research. Key findings from this research enabled an ideation session with three multi-disciplinary teams. Feasible concepts were transformed into clickable prototypes for user testing. The user testing report detailed desirability and usability of key concepts, while wider discovery insights detailed magnitude and impact. Together, this allowed successful prioritisation and backlog of help and support features.

How did we conduct discovery?

The focus on this topic was precipitated by an Ombudsman's report, so I began by extracting recommendations as they pertained to the registered experience. We collected and synthesised relevant research from other parts of the business. Analytics from the newly launched FAQ portal helped us understand what content visitors were most interested in. We listened to complaints at the call-centre and interviewed the customer service operators.

Key findings

The bulk of complaints to the Ombudsman were regarding loss, delay, and delivery issues. Delivery issues were the primary reason for complaints. In the help & support portal, the most-visited FAQS were all parcel tracking-related. Dissatisfaction with the help & support portal was evidenced by a low customer effort score of 1.67.

A majority of MyPost feedback seeking help and support concerned navigation and authentication difficulties. This feedback revealed confusion surrounding the different authentication credentials between systems, difficulty in locating the enquiry portal log in, and confusion about the global header links. Customers were being caught in a loop and were unsure whether they were logged in. 

Call-listening sessions revealed MyPost-specific calls were driven by confusion around how the product worked, cases of mismatched identity and difficulties in closing accounts. We found that customers often call when it is unclear whether fixing an issue was Auspost's responsibility or theirs (e.g. lack of track updates, closing an account, identity mismatch). 

A diagram illustrating the loop that users were trapped in.

Summary of findings

Findings from this research led to the following recommendations:

  1. Redirect logged-in users to relevant step of the enquiry process
  2. Pre-populate information when customer details are known
  3. Display open support enquiries on the logged-in (MyPost) dashboard
  4. Enable easier submission of delivery-related complaints

The creation of testable concepts

Once the data was synthesised into actionable information, we conducted an ideation session. Three multi-disciplinary teams focused on a different area of the MyPost ecosystem, each tackling a different aspect of help and support. Teams presented their top concepts, and the winning concepts were then vetted for technical feasibility.

Two concepts: helpful rating on tracking status and submit enquiry from tracking status

Taking the concepts to user testing

The winning concepts from ideation were wireframed and prototyped in anticipation of user testing sessions. Scenarios were created for users and at the end of each scenarios customers were asked to rate specific aspects of the experience. This allowed us to rate features in terms of customer desirability. Customer feedback was synthesised and insights were presented alongside a final recommendation for each feature. 

The outcome of desirability testing of two concepts

Recommendations

The outcome of the discovery process resulted in a set of next steps detailing which features + fixes should be prioritised. This took into account the technical feasibility, customer desirability and the magnitude of each feature + fix. This assisted management in deciding which features to proceed with for the following quarter.

Recommended next steps based on research, testing and feasability

Was the discovery process successful?

Yes! The dedicated research time ensured we had enough context to ensure we were focusing on the right problems. The workshop ensured we tackled the problems together, and that the larger problems were broken down into technically feasible features. The smaller features meant we could swiftly implement the Ombudsman recommendations.

A follow-up Ombudsman report, published June 2019, stated that AusPost “has made significant progress towards implementing our recommendations”, of which a key component was “making it easier for customers to lodge complaints and enquiries online”. They noted that complaints had reduced in proportion to parcel volumes for the 2017-2018 financial year.
