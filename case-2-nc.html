    Nominate To Collect

    Simplifying parcel collection by nominating a friend or partner to safely and seamlessly collect on the customers behalf.

    Australia Post hosted Hack Days in which teams would spend two days hacking together projects based around a theme. The 2018 Hack Day asked for submissions that incorporated  machine-learning and algorithms. I assisted a team of Android developers to develop a feature which integrated facial recognition in order to simplify the collection of missed deliveries.

    What was the UX problem?

    This feature centred on improving the parcel collection experience after failed delivery attempts. ‚ÄòCarding‚Äô describes a failed delivery attempt where a physical card is left at the premises. This card indicates when and where the parcel can be collected. It also allows the intended recipient to delegate the collection to another person via signature. However, customers sometimes encounter difficulties in locating their card (either because it was not left by the driver or it blew away) while others encounter difficulties when someone attempts to collect the delivery on their behalf. Carded deliveries are not a great delivery experience and are highly correlated with NPS 'detractor' ratings.

    Working in the context of a Hack Day project, I was heavily time-constrained (36 hours) so I had to move rapidly to validate the problem, find a solution, illustrate the solution, and create demonstration videos to market the project.

    Quantitative insights

    I wished to gain a high-level understanding of the scale of the carding problem. Visitor analytics revealed page visits to the carding help page was trending upwards. Page visitation increased from 19 million visits (0.07% of total visits) in 2017 to 36 million (0.13% of total visits) in 2018. Using page visits as a proxy indicated that this was a growing problem. 

    Analytics revealed visitation rates to the 'missed deliveries' page increased relative to overall site visitation.

    Meeting with the parcel collection manager gave us a third-party view on the project‚Äôs strength and limitations. Strengths including tackling the carding issue which generates significant negative media attention.     Prototyping the solution

    To rapidly bring these flows to life I used the design software Figma. HackDay was a great opportunity to test the rapid prototyping functionality. These prototypes assisted in helping the devs build out the primary nomination flow to be shown live at the Hack Day marketplace.

    Two prototypes depicting: (A) Nominating someone to collect on your behalf, and (B) Being nominated to collect. 

    What was the outcome?

    Feedback for the project was positive and we received a commendation in the closing speech but unfortunately this project did not win. Constructive criticism included that strong reliance on electronic matching meant that the overall impact may not be large enough and that the retail integration was not holistic. Ultimately, lacking a comprehensive study of the interaction between sender, receiver, the parcel-management system and retail, this project was only one step towards truly revolutionising the delivery experience. However, the research provides evidence that there is a problem, and the positive feedback indicates the seed of a great project is there.
